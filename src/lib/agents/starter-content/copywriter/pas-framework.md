# PAS - Problem, Agitate, Solve

PAS is the long-form sister of AIDA. Three beats, in order:
**Problem, Agitate, Solve**. Used for sales letters, VSLs, and any
piece of copy where the reader has time to read more than 200 words.

## Problem

Name the problem in the reader's exact words. Do not paraphrase. If
the avatar says "I cannot get past the gatekeeper", the headline does
not say "navigating organisational layers". You write the problem as
the reader writes it in private.

Sources for the exact words:

- Sales call transcripts. The first 90 seconds is where the buyer
  surfaces the pain unprompted.
- Reviews of competitors, especially the 2- and 3-star ones. Buyers
  who almost bought tell the cleanest story of the gap.
- DMs and replies on social posts where the brand asked an open
  question. People type fast and use real words.

A clean problem section is one paragraph, three sentences. State the
problem, name the consequence, finish with a question the reader is
already asking.

## Agitate

Make the cost real. The reader knows the problem; agitation is where
you show the reader the bill they have not added up yet.

Four agitation moves, ranked by how often they land:

1. **Compounding cost.** "Every month you stay on the wrong pricing
   model you give away $4,000 in margin you will never get back."
2. **Comparison.** "Your competitor with the worse product is winning
   the deals you should have closed."
3. **Time horizon.** "In 18 months the offer you sell today will not
   exist - the market is moving away from it."
4. **Identity.** "You did not build this business to be the person
   chasing $500 invoices on Friday at 9pm."

Pick one move. Stack two only when the price point justifies it. Three
or more reads as desperate.

Length: 100-200 words. Long enough to feel the weight, short enough
that the reader keeps moving.

## Solve

Drop the answer. Specific. Mechanism + result + proof, the same three
beats as the AIDA Desire section.

Then handle objections in order. The five that show up on every
purchase decision in B2B and high-ticket B2C:

- Will this actually work for me? Answer with a similar-shape case
  study, not a generic testimonial.
- Can I afford it? Answer with payback math, not with discounts.
- Is now the right time? Answer with the cost of waiting (loop back
  to agitation).
- What if it does not work? Answer with the guarantee or the risk-
  reversal mechanism.
- How is this different from X? Answer with the one structural
  difference, not five surface differences.

Close with the offer. State price clearly. Then state the CTA in one
verb.

## The PAS audit

Run any sales letter through this checklist before it ships:

- [ ] Problem section uses the avatar's exact words
- [ ] Agitate section uses one move, max two
- [ ] Solve section has mechanism + result + proof
- [ ] Top five objections are handled in order
- [ ] Price is stated, not hidden
- [ ] CTA is one verb tied to one URL
