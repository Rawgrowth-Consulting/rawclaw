# AIDA - the four-beat copy framework

AIDA is the oldest workhorse in direct response. Four beats, in order:
**Attention, Interest, Desire, Action**. Every short-form ad you write
collapses into these four moves. If you cannot point at the line that
does each beat, the ad is broken.

## Attention

The hook. One line. Earns the next line and nothing else. Three patterns
that work on cold traffic in 2025:

1. **Pattern interrupt.** Open with a fact the reader did not expect.
   "Most founders price their offer 40 percent below what the market
   would pay."
2. **Direct callout.** Name the avatar in the first six words. "Solo
   consultants billing under $20k per month: read this before you
   raise rates."
3. **Specific number.** Use one concrete number, not a range. "$3,847
   in 11 days from a one-page funnel."

Write five hooks for every ad. Pick one. Save the other four for
variants and split tests.

## Interest

Hold the reader. One short paragraph that tees up the desire by naming
the cost of the current state. Do not pitch yet. Show the reader you
understand the problem better than the reader can articulate it.

The structure is **before / cost / what changes**:

> Before: most coaches book sales calls from cold DMs.
> Cost: 80 percent of those calls no-show, the rest haggle on price.
> What changes: when the call is booked through a paid mini-offer the
> show rate jumps to 95 and price haggling drops to zero.

## Desire

Show the better state. Specific, not abstract. Use proof.

Three proof beats that move buyers in this order: **mechanism, result,
case**.

- Mechanism: one sentence that explains why the offer works. Not the
  full system, the gist. Buyers do not buy what they cannot picture.
- Result: a measurable outcome with a timeline. "8 booked calls in 14
  days, no cold outreach."
- Case: a named client, not a faceless testimonial. Three lines max.

## Action

One CTA. One verb. One destination.

- "Book the demo." Not "Click here to learn more about our solutions."
- "Get the playbook." Not "Sign up to gain access."
- "Reply YES." Not "Take the next step on your journey."

Match the CTA to the awareness stage. Cold traffic gets a low-friction
CTA (read, watch, download). Warm traffic gets the offer CTA (book,
buy, schedule). Never ask cold traffic to book a sales call as the
first move.

## The 60-second AIDA audit

Run any ad through this checklist before it ships:

- [ ] Attention line stands alone (no context needed)
- [ ] Interest paragraph names the cost in concrete terms
- [ ] Desire section has mechanism + result + case
- [ ] Action is a single verb tied to a single URL
- [ ] No banned brand-voice words, no em-dashes
- [ ] Reading level is sixth grade or lower

If two of those fail, the ad is not ready. Rewrite the worst section
first, never the cleanest.
