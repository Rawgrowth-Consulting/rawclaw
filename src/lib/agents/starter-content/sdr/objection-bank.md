# Objection bank - the five that show up on every cadence

Five objections cover 90 percent of outbound replies. For each: how it
sounds, what it actually means, and the one-line response that keeps
the conversation alive.

## 1. "Not now"

How it sounds:
- "Reach out in Q3."
- "We are heads-down on something else right now."
- "Bad timing."

What it means: the prospect either does not see the cost of waiting
clearly enough to break the current priority, or they are politely
exiting.

Response - one sentence. Never push, always offer the cost of waiting
once.

> Understood - the teams who push this to Q3 typically lose another
> $200k in pipeline coverage by end of year, so worth keeping the
> door open. Should I circle back in 60 days?

This works because it adds a number, it does not argue, and it asks
permission. The prospect will either confirm 60 days (warm exit) or
escalate (which means the cost line landed).

## 2. "No budget"

How it sounds:
- "We do not have budget this year."
- "Capex is frozen."
- "We are cost-cutting."

What it means: usually one of three. Either the buyer does not own
the budget line, or the offer has not been priced against the cost of
not solving the problem, or the prospect is deflecting to end the
call.

Response - one sentence. Reframe budget as cost of the problem.

> Got it - what is the current cost of the problem on the team
> annually? Most of the customers we close find the cost outpaces the
> investment by 5-10x in year one, which is when budget conversations
> usually unlock.

If the prospect engages with the cost question, the objection was
real. If the prospect deflects again, exit cleanly.

## 3. "Send info"

How it sounds:
- "Just send me a deck."
- "Email me a one-pager."
- "I will look it over and get back to you."

What it means: 80 percent of "send info" requests are exits. The
prospect wants the call over without being rude. The remaining 20
percent are real - usually a buyer who needs ammunition to brief
internally.

Response - one sentence. Diagnose which type before sending anything.

> Happy to. To send the right one-pager, what is the specific question
> you need answered for the internal conversation - pricing, the
> implementation ask, or the ROI math?

A real buyer answers with one of the three. An exit goes silent. Do
not send a deck to an exit; nothing in a deck wins back a prospect
who has already left.

## 4. "Already using competitor"

How it sounds:
- "We use [Competitor] already."
- "We just signed with [Competitor]."
- "[Competitor] does this for us."

What it means: depends on tenure. New users (under 6 months) are
locked in by switching cost; the right play is to schedule a follow-
up at renewal. Old users (over 18 months) are usually frustrated and
ready to talk if the angle is right.

Response - one sentence. Probe tenure first, never trash competitor.

> [Competitor] is a solid choice. How long have you been on it, and
> is there one thing about the current setup you wish were different?

If the prospect names a frustration, you have an opening. If the
prospect says "no, all good" you exit and re-enter at renewal.

## 5. "Gatekeeper"

How it sounds:
- Email auto-reply from an EA / chief of staff: "I handle [name]'s
  inbox - what is this regarding?"
- "I am not the right person, you want [name]."

What it means: the gatekeeper is doing the job. Argue and you lose
the relationship; comply and you may get the warm intro.

Response - two-line email, never push past the gate.

> Thank you - I am reaching out about pipeline coverage at [Co]. If
> [name] is not the right person, would you point me at who owns
> outbound on the team?
>
> Happy to share a one-page overview first if that helps the routing.

EAs route requests well when treated as professionals. Pushing past
them burns the door for the next 12 months.

## What this file is for

When the prospect replies with an objection, match it to the bank
above. If the reply does not match any pattern, the prospect is
giving real signal - reply to the actual content, not from a script.
